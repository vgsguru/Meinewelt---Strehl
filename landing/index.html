Chapter 01 · The Challenge

Turbulence corrupts the wavefront faster than you can react.

Atmospheric turbulence distorts an incoming plane wavefront on a millisecond timescale. A Shack-Hartmann sensor samples it as a grid of moving spots — but by the time you measure, reconstruct, and command the mirror, the atmosphere has already changed. That residual is servo-lag error, the dominant limit on sharpness.

Milliseconds matter

The coherence time τ₀ is only a few ms. Corrections must outrun it — a hard real-time, computational-efficiency constraint.

Sub-pixel spots

Each lenslet's spot shifts by a fraction of a pixel. Recovery demands precise centroiding and a well-conditioned reconstruction.

Characterise the sky

From the same data, derive the Fried parameter r₀ and coherence time τ₀ — the physical fingerprints of the turbulence overhead.

Chapter 02 · Our Solution

Don't just react — predict.

We satisfy the brief with classical zonal + modal reconstruction, then go beyond it: a predictive control layer forecasts each Zernike mode ahead in time, feeding the mirror the wavefront it will face — not the one that already passed. Grounded in real AO research (Gemini ALTAIR linear forecasting), not an ML bolt-on.

Chapter 03 · How We Solved It

Calibrate once. Then it's just matrix-vector multiplies.

Every calibration matrix — reconstruction, prediction, DM influence — is built a single time at startup. The per-frame real-time path collapses to a few small matvecs plus a JIT-compiled centroider, so the loop runs far faster than the turbulence evolves.

Folded control matrix

Reconstruction × actuator projection pre-multiply into one slopes→strokes matrix — "direct gradient control," as SPHERE/Keck run their fast path.

JIT hot loop

The centroider is numba-compiled and parallel over sub-apertures — a 14× speed-up over vectorised NumPy on the only non-trivial per-frame cost.

Validated at every step

Synthetic ground truth at each phase: forward-model exactness, the Noll residual trend, r₀/τ₀ recovery — every claim is a number, not a slogan.

Chapter 04 · Why Ours Is Better

The predictor holds sharpness where the reactive loop collapses.

As loop delay grows, a conventional integrator's correction goes stale and the Strehl ratio falls off a cliff. The predictive layer extrapolates the frozen-flow motion and stays sharp — exactly the regime servo-lag hurts most.
